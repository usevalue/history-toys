Credit is due

This calendar was developed as part of a wider personal research effort on the Later Han Dynasty. It takes the translation of Cui Shi's 2nd century Simin Yueling (or Ts'ui's Ssu-Min-Yüeh-Ling, following the Wade-Giles convention of the translation) found in Cho-yun Hsu's "Han Agriculture: The Formation of Early Chinese Agrarian Economy (206 B.C. - A.D. 220)" and remixes it as an interactive calendar. The headings (A.3, D.4) are Cho-yun Hsu's, with the exception of subheadings I introduced to divide longer passages into distinct instructions, (e.g. A.1.a., A.1.b...). The individual categories were influenced by Patricia Ebrey's "Estate and Family Management in the Later Han as Seen in the Monthly Instructions for the Four Classes of People" but the final schema is original to this work.

Production of the Calendar

The preparation of this calendar was heavily aided by Google Gemini and it is appropriate to talk about the software stack I used in production. An LLM model is not on its own a tool and I used Gemini in three distinct capacities: as an analyst/advisor, as a data processor, and as a coder. My initial interest in Cui Shi, discovery of the existince and significance of the Simin Yueling, owe to conversations with the Gemini chat bot. Gemini wrote prompts for other instances which then categorized each entry in Hsu's translation into one of several buckets based on Ebrey's section headings. This process iterated several times as a new schema was worked out ("Rites and Sacrifices," "Health Measures," and "Commercial Activities" are the remaining direct borrowings) and the rules for subdividing longer sections were worked out. Finally, I used Gemini to code the front end in VS-Code.

Results and conclusions

The purpose of this work was to take a 2nd century farmer's almanac and make it into something visual and tactile, with the need to click and scroll. The categorization itself was an interesting process and I won't vigorously defend every classification. I insisted to some instances Gemini that when the master reverently prepares a product for ritual sacrifice, that's a ritual task and not a regular productivity task. But I did not in most instances vet the sorting performed by the LLM. What I will say is to an extent the Simin Yueling defies these neat classifications, with the exception of commercial transactions, which, Ebrey notes, Cui Shi is usually eager to leave to a discrete entry at the end of a month's activities. In the fifth month, midsummer, Hsu's translation breaks down into five of the six categories (nothing specific to social and clan life which is not categorized under rites and sacrifices; summer is busy agriculturally). Two of those categories mention the forces of Yin and Yang explicitly: the decrease of Yang forces and the warming of the weather after the solstice is the best time to air out and relax bows and bowstrings. The contention of Yin and Yang around the time of the solstice makes this a time of separate beds for the patriarch and his wife; I've followed Ebrey in calling this a health measure. The explicit ritual measures for the solstice include a sacrifice to the ancestors which must be preceded by purification. Here "ritual" means only the most tightly prescribed actions rather than the social world which is still often interpreted through a metaphysical lens. Similarly, visiting relatives and educating the young are filed under "social and clan life" though they obviously have implications and resonances across ritual, economic, and even military spheres. Older children study the Confucian classics in the early spring, but younger children get additional instruction in the fall. Presumably in the fall the older children are helping to restring the bows in preparation to participate in the hunts and military exercises. No one cleanly lived their life in one sphere or another. Nevertheless, you can to some extent track the visual ebb and flow. As you click through the months, note when the agricultural and labor activities section is busy versus when it is quiet. The Simin Yueling is literally the "Monthly Instructions for the Four Classes of People" so see if you can track where people on the estate go during the quiet times of the agricultural year.
